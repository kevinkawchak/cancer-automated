# Oncology Trial VVUQ and Emerging LLM Pipelines

**Digital twins and VVUQ frameworks.** Digital twins – patient-specific computational models – are poised to revolutionize oncology trials by simulating individual treatment outcomes. Ensuring their **verification, validation, and uncertainty quantification (VVUQ)** is critical for patient safety and efficacy. Recent reviews emphasize that oncology digital twins integrate multi-omics and clinical data to predict tumor growth and therapy response, but face challenges in data integration, model validation, and scalability. For example, a narrative review notes that "challenges persist in data integration, model validation, [and] computational scalability" for oncology digital twins. Another synthesis of VVUQ principles highlights that while many digital twin models exist, *formal model verification* is rarely documented in publications, and dynamic twins require ongoing validation and uncertainty tracking (re-validation frequency, iterative testing) beyond traditional static models. In summary, **VVUQ ensures AI-driven trial models are fit-for-purpose**: models must be verified to correctly implement mathematical formulations, validated against real-world outcomes, and have uncertainties quantified (e.g. via Bayesian methods).

**LLMs for trial data operations.** A growing number of oncology trial data tasks are being addressed with large language models (LLMs). For example, **eligibility criteria conversion** can be automated: a recent system uses GPT-4 to transform free-text inclusion/exclusion criteria into OMOP-CDM database queries. This LLM pipeline achieved 48.5% mapping accuracy (versus 32% for a rule-based tool) and 45% effective SQL generation (compared to 75% by a smaller LLM variant, due to fewer hallucinations). The study found LLM "hallucination" rates (false medical codes) of ~21–50%, with wrong domain mapping and placeholders common. Such errors "can severely compromise query accuracy and patient safety" unless carefully validated. Similarly, a **multimodal LLM pipeline for patient-to-trial matching** was validated on real oncology data. Callies *et al.* (2025) showed an LLM-based system directly consuming raw EHR documents could match patients to trials, achieving ~93% accuracy on a benchmark and 87% on a 36-site oncology trial dataset. This pipeline cut chart-review time by ~80% (to <9 minutes per patient) and required no site-specific integration, demonstrating practical gains in recruitment efficiency. Other use cases include LLM-assisted **data extraction and summarization** (e.g. auto-generating discharge summaries from clinical data) and AE detection: one case study found ChatGPT matched human annotators on adverse event identification (F1 ≈94% any-AE, 99% serious-AE). In brief, LLMs are being deployed to automate eligibility screening, data harmonization, and reporting in trials.

**Validation metrics and UQ practices.** Published pipelines report rigorous evaluation metrics. The patient-matching system was tested on both public and private datasets, logging sensitivity/specificity and end-to-end latency. The query generation study exhaustively compared 8 LLMs, reporting concept mapping accuracy (48–73% by category) and SQL generation "effective rates". It explicitly evaluated *hallucinations* by analyzing 760 prompts and found overall 32.7% hallucination rate. Such work underlines the need for **systematic validation of AI outputs** – for example, by checking LLM queries against known reference sets or by expert review. Uncertainty quantification is emerging: one novel method (SPUQ) perturbs prompts and samples multiple LLM outputs to capture both aleatoric and epistemic uncertainty. SPUQ reduced expected calibration error by ~50% on average, showing promise for making LLM results more reliable. While most clinical-AI studies focus on accuracy, these examples signal attention to reliability: e.g. explicitly designed prompt strategies can encourage LLMs to flag uncertainty (using placeholders), and hybrid pipelines may combine LLM strengths with rule-based checks to mitigate errors.

**Regulatory context.** Regulatory frameworks are adapting to AI in trials. FDA's draft "AI Credibility" guidance (Jan 2025) prescribes a risk-based framework requiring sponsors to pre-define each AI model's context of use and credibility factors (data quality, model verification/validation, monitoring). Likewise, the Jan 2026 FDA/EMA guiding principles mandate human-centric design, clear context of use, rigorous model documentation, and risk-based performance assessment. For digital twins and synthetic controls, regulators stress that external/computational control arms need the same evidentiary rigor as traditional controls, especially for complex AI-generated data. A position paper also notes that broad adoption of digital twins in trials hinges on demonstrating trust through rigorous validation and compliance. In practice this means any LLM-based trial pipeline must be accompanied by robust VVUQ evidence: e.g. comparison to standard methods, error rate documentation, bias analysis, and ongoing monitoring.

**Summary:** Recent literature (2024–2026) demonstrates that *AI-driven pipelines* can dramatically accelerate oncology trial data operations, but VVUQ remains essential. State-of-the-art LLM systems have been **validated on real oncology data**, showing high accuracy in matching and query generation. However, these methods still exhibit non-trivial error/hallucination rates (often >20%), underscoring the need for rigorous model selection, prompt strategy, and human oversight. Regulatory bodies now demand transparency and context-specific testing for any AI model in drug development. In conclusion, **LLM-enhanced VVUQ pipelines** offer a path toward faster, more scalable trial analytics, but will only earn their place by demonstrably improving on existing methods through thorough validation and uncertainty analysis.
