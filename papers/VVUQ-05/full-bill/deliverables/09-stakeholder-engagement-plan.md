# Stakeholder Engagement and Outreach Plan: H. R. 9510, the Verification Before Generation in Physical AI Oncology Trials Act of 2026

*Independent research draft. Not an enacted law, not pending legislation, and not legal advice; not endorsed by the FDA, HHS, any standards body, or any Member of Congress.*

**Bill:** H. R. 9510 (illustrative number; the Clerk assigns the real number at introduction), 119th Congress, 2d Session. **Amends:** the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, 21 U.S.C. § 301 et seq. **Refer to:** House Committee on Energy and Commerce (Subcommittee on Health). **Scope of this document:** the Phase 2 stakeholder engagement step that precedes any sponsor's introduction.

## 1. Goals and principles

The bill's operative rule is that no robot-patient interaction code may be generated or executed in a Physical AI oncology investigation unless an automated verification, validation, and uncertainty quantification (VVUQ) process, bound to named external consensus standards, has first cleared. That mechanism makes engagement unusually concrete: because the gate is bound to specific published standards, the bodies that author and maintain those standards are the natural technical reviewers of whether the bindings are stated correctly. Outreach therefore has four goals: (1) test the standards bindings for accuracy and currency with the people who wrote them; (2) surface implementation burden and feasibility concerns early, while the text can still change; (3) build informed, voluntary support among the communities the bill affects; and (4) keep a faithful, traceable record of who was consulted and what they said.

Five principles govern every contact. First, accuracy before advocacy: lead with the text and the evidence, not with persuasion. Second, no claimed endorsement: no group has endorsed this bill, and no message, slide, or email may imply otherwise. Third, listen to revise: treat objections as inputs to the draft, not obstacles to it. Fourth, transparency of origin: the draft was AI-assisted, and that is disclosed up front so a named human owner can take responsibility for the final text. Fifth, respect for role: standards bodies set consensus standards and do not legislate, agencies administer the law and do not author bills, and patient groups speak for patients; the plan never asks any party to act outside its role.

## 3. Sequencing and timeline

Engagement runs as a sequenced wave inside calendar year 2026, because the 119th Congress, 2d Session ends January 3, 2027, and a draft not introduced and acted on by then must be reintroduced.

- Weeks 1 to 3, preparation. Finalize the briefing package (one-page summary, section-by-section, plain-English memo, findings, and Ramseyer print), confirm each standards citation against the most current edition, and stand up the tracking log in Section 6.
- Weeks 3 to 8, technical review with standards bodies. Approach ASME first, given V&V 40 and VVUQ 70 are the keystone bindings, then IEC and ISO, then IEEE, NASA, UL, and the AMA. These contacts are corrective in purpose: confirm the bindings are accurate before anyone is asked to support the text.
- Weeks 6 to 12, agency and community briefings. Brief FDA (CDRH) and HHS on administrability; in parallel, brief patient-advocacy and oncology groups, industry, and academic and professional societies.
- Weeks 10 to 16, consolidation. Fold feedback into a single revised text, document what changed and why, and confirm that the standards bindings still resolve after edits.
- Weeks 14 onward, handoff. Provide the consolidated text and the engagement record to a prospective House sponsor's office, with all disclaimers intact.

Standards-body review precedes the support-building briefings on purpose: the bill stands or falls on whether its external anchors are stated correctly, so that has to be verified before the text is socialized more broadly.

## 4. Key messages by audience

To FDA and HHS: this is a sequencing rule, not a new approval pathway; it complements premarket review, the § 515C change-control authority, and the quality-system and record-integrity frameworks already in force, and it asks the agency only for the administrability read that a new § 515D would require.

To the standards bodies (ASME, IEC, ISO, IEEE, NASA, UL, AMA): the bill leans on your published work and binds each gate criterion to a named standard, so the request is technical accuracy, please confirm we have characterized your standards, editions, and development status correctly, and tell us where a citation is wrong or stale. ASME's V&V 40 and the in-development VVUQ 70 are the central anchors; the request is not endorsement but correctness.

To patient and oncology groups: the rule exists so that verification comes before an irreversible robotic motion, and the question is where patient disclosure and protection should be stronger.

To industry: the rule is implementable on today's evidence and is anchored to standards you already meet; help us confirm the transition periods are workable.

To academic and professional societies: the bill is built on consensus methods and reporting guidance your members publish; help us keep the methodology current.

## 5. Risks and how to keep outreach respectful

The principal risk is implied endorsement. The bill names many organizations, and any of them could reasonably worry that being briefed looks like backing the bill. Mitigation: every contact opens and closes with the standing disclaimer, the deck carries it on the title slide, and follow-up email repeats that no endorsement is sought or implied. A second risk is misstating a standard, which would be both inaccurate and disrespectful to the body that authored it; mitigation is the corrective framing of Section 3, where standards bodies are asked to correct the bindings before the text is advocated, plus a final pre-handoff citation check. A third risk is overstepping role, for example asking an agency to take a position on pending-style legislation it should not comment on; mitigation is to confine each ask to what fits that party's role, as in the stakeholder map. A fourth risk is opacity about origin; mitigation is to disclose the AI-assisted drafting and the named human owner in the first contact. A final risk is fatigue or pressure from repeated outreach; mitigation is a single consolidated package, one primary contact per organization, and no second approach unless invited. Throughout, disagreement is recorded faithfully and reflected in the text rather than argued away.

## 6. Tracking

Engagement is logged so the record is auditable and so the disclaimers stay true. Each row records the organization, the named contact and role, the standards or sections in scope, the date and channel, the ask made, the substantive response, the resulting text change (if any), and the open-item status.

| Field | What it captures |
|:--|:--|
| Organization and contact | Stakeholder, named person, and role |
| Scope | Standards keys or bill sections discussed (for example V&V 40, § 515D) |
| Date and channel | When and how contact occurred |
| Ask and response | The specific request and the substantive reply |
| Disposition | Text change made, deferred, or none, with reason |
| Endorsement status | Confirmed: no endorsement sought or given |
| Open items | Follow-ups outstanding and owner |

Two log invariants hold at every review: no row ever records a claimed endorsement, and every standards correction is either reflected in the text or carries a written reason it was not. The completed log accompanies the consolidated text at handoff to a prospective sponsor, so the office inherits both the draft and a faithful account of who was consulted and what they said.
