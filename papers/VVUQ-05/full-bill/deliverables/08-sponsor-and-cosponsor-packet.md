## Purpose of this packet

This packet is the recruitment and sign-on instrument for the draft bill. In one place it tells a prospective sponsoring office what role the bill needs filled, what each signer is being asked to do, where the measure is expected to be referred, and what must be in hand before introduction. It does not introduce anything. Only a sitting Member of the House can introduce a House bill; an outside research project cannot file it directly. The objective is therefore singular: place the finished text in the hands of one Member who will introduce it, and gather original cosponsors to accompany it.

The substantive companion documents travel with this packet: the one-page summary, the section-by-section analysis, the plain-English policy memo, the legislative findings, the Ramseyer comparative print, and the Constitutional Authority Statement. This packet is the cover under which those materials are circulated for sign-on.

## The sponsor (role and what is needed)

A House bill has exactly one sponsor: the Member who introduces it and whose name leads the bill. Every other supporter is a cosponsor. The sponsor's office owns the introduced text and is the contact for the Clerk, the Parliamentarian, and the committee of referral.

Two items are required from the sponsor before introduction:

- The sponsor's signature on the bill. Under current cloakroom guidance the sponsoring Member's signature is required on the measure itself.
- A signed Constitutional Authority Statement (CAS). Under Rule XII, clause 7(c)(1), a bill may not be introduced unless the sponsor submits a statement citing, as specifically as practicable, the constitutional power to enact it. For this measure the citation is Article I, Section 8, Clause 3 (the Commerce Clause), on which the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act rests, supported by Clause 18 (the Necessary and Proper Clause). The statement is printed in the Congressional Record and made available electronically by the Clerk.

Best-fit sponsor profile: a Member of the House Committee on Energy and Commerce, ideally on the Subcommittee on Health, which holds FDA and FD&C Act jurisdiction, with a demonstrated interest in medical-device safety, oncology care, or trustworthy AI. The sponsoring office is also expected to route the final text through the House Office of the Legislative Counsel for proper form before filing.

## Original cosponsors (form and target profile)

Original cosponsors are Members listed at the time of introduction, distinct from cosponsors added afterward. A public bill may have an unlimited number of cosponsors, and under Rule XII the sponsor names them. Bipartisan original cosponsors meaningfully improve a bill's prospects; recruiting at least one Member from each party is a deliberate goal of this packet. To be counted as original, a Member must commit before or at introduction; once the bill is filed, the original-cosponsor list closes and later supporters are added through the eHopper. Circulate for sign-on only after the sponsor approves the exact introduction version, so every signer endorses identical text.

Target profile for original cosponsors (a balanced slate spans all three interests, on both sides of the aisle):

- Device and FDA interest: Members on Energy and Commerce with medical-device or FDA-oversight engagement.
- Oncology and patient-safety interest: Members focused on cancer care, clinical-trial integrity, and patient protection.
- AI and technology interest: Members engaged on artificial intelligence, autonomy, and emerging technology, plausibly including members of the Committee on Science, Space, and Technology.

## Committee referral expectation

Expect primary referral to the House Committee on Energy and Commerce (Subcommittee on Health), which holds jurisdiction over the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act and FDA device authorities. Because the bill addresses machine-generated robot-patient interaction code and the autonomy of Physical AI systems, a secondary referral to the Committee on Science, Space, and Technology is possible. The Speaker, advised by the Parliamentarian, makes the referral after introduction. The sponsor's staff may ask the Parliamentarian to review the draft in advance for likely referral and parliamentary problems.

## The introduction checklist

- [ ] One authoritative introduction version of the text is selected, frozen, and version-controlled; no further edits to the introduced version.
- [ ] A named human attorney and qualified oncology/regulatory experts have reviewed and taken ownership of the final text.
- [ ] Currency check completed: confirm no newer public law has altered Title 21, that the target section number (515D / 21 U.S.C. § 360e-5) remains unused, and that every cross-reference and conforming amendment still lands.
- [ ] Final text routed through the House Office of the Legislative Counsel for proper legislative form.
- [ ] Parliamentarian review requested (through the sponsor's staff) for likely referral and parliamentary issues.
- [ ] Sponsor identified and committed; sponsor's signature obtained on the bill.
- [ ] Constitutional Authority Statement finalized, signed, and ready to submit with the measure (Art. I, § 8, cl. 3, supported by cl. 18).
- [ ] Original cosponsor list collected on the exact introduction version, with bipartisan balance pursued.
- [ ] Companion materials assembled: one-pager, section-by-section, policy memo, findings, Ramseyer print.
- [ ] PAYGO statement and earmark declaration prepared as applicable (the bill directs rulemaking only and is expected to be PAYGO neutral).
- [ ] Filing logistics confirmed: submission via the eHopper, the House's primary electronic introduction mechanism (accepted from about 15 minutes before convening through about 15 minutes after adjournment). Staff cannot drop documents in the physical Floor Hopper under current guidance.
- [ ] AI-assisted origin disclosed to the sponsoring office (see the transparency note below).
