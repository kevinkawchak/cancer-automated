# Constitutional Authority Statement: H. R. 9510, the Verification Before Generation in Physical AI Oncology Trials Act of 2026

*Independent research draft. Not an enacted law, not pending legislation, and not legal advice; not endorsed by the FDA, HHS, or any Member of Congress.*

**Bill:** H. R. 9510 (illustrative number; the Clerk assigns the real number at introduction), 119th Congress, 2d Session. **Amends:** the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, 21 U.S.C. § 301 et seq. **Requirement:** Rule XII, clause 7(c)(1), of the Rules of the House of Representatives.

## 1. The formal statement

Congress has the power to enact this legislation pursuant to the following: Article I, Section 8, Clause 3, of the Constitution of the United States (the Commerce Clause), which empowers Congress to regulate commerce among the several States, as supplemented by Article I, Section 8, Clause 18 (the Necessary and Proper Clause).

This single sentence is the operative submission. The sections below explain it and describe how it is filed; they are not part of the statement printed under clause 7(c)(1).

## 2. Why the Commerce Clause

The Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act has rested on the Commerce Clause since its enactment in 1938, and the Act's prohibited-acts provisions (21 U.S.C. § 331) are written expressly in terms of articles introduced into, or held for sale after shipment in, interstate commerce. Because H. R. 9510 amends that Act, by adding a new section 515D (codified at 21 U.S.C. § 360e-5) and ten conforming amendments threaded through Title 21, it draws on the same constitutional source as the statute it modifies. The bill adds no new jurisdictional theory; it extends a recognized one to a new class of regulated articles.

Medical devices are quintessential articles of interstate commerce. Surgical and oncology robots, their components, and the software that governs robot-patient interaction are manufactured, marketed, shipped, and used across State lines, and the device authorities the bill builds on (classification under 21 U.S.C. § 360c, premarket review under 21 U.S.C. § 360e and § 360(k), and predetermined change control plans under 21 U.S.C. § 360e-4) all regulate that interstate market. The robot-patient interaction code that the bill subjects to verification before generation is itself a traded, updatable article: it is distributed to clinical-investigation sites in multiple States, modified through interstate software pipelines, and integrated into devices that move in commerce. Such code and the Physical AI systems that execute it are both instrumentalities and articles of interstate commerce, squarely within the regulatory reach of Clause 3.

The bill also reaches activity that, in the aggregate, substantially affects interstate commerce. Physical AI oncology clinical investigations are conducted by sponsors and sites operating in a national research market, generating data, evidence, and devices that enter interstate channels and inform nationwide marketing and Medicare coverage determinations. Setting a uniform sequencing rule, that automated verification, validation, and uncertainty quantification must clear before robot-patient code is generated or executed, is a regulation of the terms on which these articles and services may be placed in that market. The Necessary and Proper Clause supports the means chosen: the documentation, attestation, audit-trail, cybersecurity, and human-oversight duties in section 515D are appropriate and plainly adapted to making the underlying device and commerce regulation effective.

## 3. The Rule XII requirement and eHopper submission

Under Rule XII, clause 7(c)(1), of the Rules of the House of Representatives, a bill or joint resolution may not be introduced unless the sponsor submits, for printing in the Congressional Record, a statement citing as specifically as practicable the power or powers granted to Congress in the Constitution to enact the measure. The Clerk of the House makes each such statement available in electronic form to the public.

In practice, the statement accompanies the measure at the point of introduction rather than as a separate later filing. The House has modernized intake through the electronic hopper (the eHopper), and the Clerk testified in 2026 that sponsoring offices can generate and submit the constitutional authority statement, together with the measure, within the eHopper. For H. R. 9510, the statement to submit is the single sentence in section 1 above: a citation to Article I, Section 8, Clause 3, with Clause 18 noted as supporting. The table below summarizes the requirement.
