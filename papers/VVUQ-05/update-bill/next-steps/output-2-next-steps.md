## output-2-next-steps

Next steps before submitting a new U.S. House “H.R.” bill in 2026

As of 2026, the House is in the 119th Congress. A draft is not formally an “H.R.” bill until it is introduced and the Clerk assigns it an H.R. number. Before that point, treat it as a draft House bill intended for introduction. The House Rules Committee identifies the current House Rules as the rules for the 119th Congress, and Rule XII remains the key rule for receipt, referral, sponsorship, cosponsors, and constitutional authority statements.  

Pre-submission checklist

1. Select the final draft for introduction

For multiple drafts, reconcile them into one “introduction version.” Preserve a redline, version history, and explanation of unresolved policy choices, but submit only the final text approved by the sponsor’s office.

Confirm that the draft is in the correct legislative form. Bills and joint resolutions can become law after passage by both chambers and presentment to the President; simple and concurrent resolutions generally do not become law.  

2. Route the draft through the House Office of Legislative Counsel

The House Office of Legislative Counsel provides nonpartisan, impartial, confidential drafting services to House Members and committees, not directly to the public. If the proposal originates outside Congress, the practical step is to work through a House Member’s office.  

Ask Legislative Counsel to review the final draft for legislative form, amendatory instructions, cross-references, definitions, effective dates, authorization language, and whether the bill should amend positive-law U.S. Code text or the underlying statute. HOLC’s drafting guide notes that positive-law status affects whether a bill should amend the U.S. Code provision or the underlying statute.  

3. Get procedural and jurisdictional review before filing

Ask the House Parliamentarian’s office, usually through the sponsor’s staff, to review the draft for likely referral, House Rules issues, and possible parliamentary problems. HOLC states that the Parliamentarian can review drafts on request, identify parliamentary problems, suggest fixes, and advise on likely committee referral.  

This is especially important if the draft touches appropriations, taxes, tariffs, budget enforcement, congressional earmarks, or multiple committee jurisdictions. Current House rules include restrictions and points of order for matters such as tax/tariff measures and earmark-related disclosures before consideration.  

4. Confirm the sponsor and original cosponsors

A House bill has one sponsor; other Members may be listed as cosponsors. House Rule XII allows the sponsor of a public bill or resolution to name cosponsors, and House guidance says a public bill may have an unlimited number of cosponsors.  

Before submission, collect the original cosponsor list in the format required by the Clerk/eHopper workflow or cloakroom guidance. If the draft has several policy versions, do not circulate for cosponsors until the sponsor has approved the exact introduction version.

5. Prepare the Constitutional Authority Statement

A House bill or joint resolution may not be introduced unless the sponsor submits a statement citing, as specifically as practicable, the constitutional power or powers for Congress to enact it. The statement is printed in the Congressional Record and made publicly available electronically by the Clerk.  

The House Rules Committee’s current Constitutional Authority Statement page and form language are tied to Rule XII, clause 7(c)(1).  

For 2026 practice, note that the Clerk testified in March 2026 that House staff can generate and submit Constitutional Authority Statements within the eHopper, which was introduced to streamline compliance with House Rules.  

6. Decide whether to use 2026 eHopper/GPO workflow options

The House’s electronic filing workflow is now central to bill introduction. The Republican Cloakroom’s 2026 guidance states that Members and staff may submit legislation and additional cosponsors through eHopper, and that submissions are accepted only 15 minutes before convening, during session, and 15 minutes after adjournment.  

For HOLC-drafted measures, ask the sponsor’s staff whether to use the current option to waive GPO proofreading. The Clerk testified in 2026 that Members can waive GPO proofreading services for introduced measures drafted by HOLC, which can make legislation available on Congress.gov more quickly.  

7. Final sponsor sign-off

Before filing, confirm that the sponsor has approved:

The final bill text; the Constitutional Authority Statement; the original cosponsor list; any staff authorization required by the eHopper process; and whether the filing will be made electronically or through the physical Floor Hopper.

Current cloakroom guidance says the sponsoring Member’s signature is required on the bill and that bills and joint resolutions require a signed Constitutional Authority Statement. It also notes that staff cannot physically drop documents in the Floor Hopper under the current submission guidance, while Members and staff may submit electronically through eHopper.  

What happens after submission

Once introduced, the Clerk assigns the bill its legislative number, the Speaker refers it to the appropriate committee with assistance from the Parliamentarian, and the bill is printed in introduced form.  

After introduction, the introduced text should be treated as fixed for that bill version. If changes are needed later, they generally proceed through a new introduced bill, an amendment, a committee substitute, or other legislative vehicle rather than quietly replacing the filed draft.
