Next Steps Before a House Bill Can Be Submitted to Congress
What's in the repo, and what "submission" actually means
The papers/ directory carries the proposal through two bill forms — VVUQ‑03, a standalone VVUQ Physical AI Oncology Trial Bill, and VVUQ‑04, the same proposal recast as an amendment to the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (a new § 515D / 21 U.S.C. § 360e‑5, current through Public Law 119‑93), titled the Verification Before Generation in Physical AI Oncology Trials Act of 2026. Both are styled "H. R. 9510."
Two reality checks frame everything below:
1. "H. R. 9510" is illustrative, not real. A bill has no number until it is introduced. The Clerk assigns the numberat the moment of introduction — you cannot pre‑assign it. Treat the styled number as a placeholder.
2. A private project cannot "submit a bill to Congress" directly. Only a sitting Member of the House can introduce a House bill (agencies can transmit draft bills via an executive‑communication letter, but that route isn't available to an outside research project). So the real goal of every step below is: get the text into final form and into the hands of a Member who will introduce it.
The repo's own README already states these are drafts that "a VVUQ gate and a human reviewer must clear," and that they are "not endorsed by the FDA, HHS, the OLRC, CFR, ICH, or any member of Congress." Those disclaimers define the work that remains.

Phase 1 — Settle on one authoritative text
Because you have multiple drafts, the first task is to stop having multiple drafts:
* Pick the vehicle. The VVUQ‑04 FD&C Act amendment is the more introduction‑ready form — it edits existing law with specific amendatory instructions ("Section 515D… is amended by…") and conforming amendments, which is how device legislation is actually written and referred. The VVUQ‑03 standalone bill is better used as the explanatory/findings backbone. Decide which is the bill and which is supporting material; don't introduce both.
* Freeze and version the text. Choose the final full-bill text, lock it, and stop editing the introduced version after this point.
* Human subject‑matter + legal review. Have qualified counsel and oncology/regulatory experts review the substance — this is the "human reviewer clears the gate" step the repo itself requires.
* Currency check. The draft is current "through Public Law 119‑93 / May 31, 2026." Re‑verify, immediately before introduction, that no newer public law has changed Title 21, that the target section number (§ 360e‑5 / § 515D) is still unused, and that every cross‑reference and each of the ten conforming amendments still lands correctly.

Phase 2 — Secure a sponsor and build support
* Find a sponsoring Representative. Identify Members with jurisdiction interest — most naturally on the House Energy and Commerce Committee (which holds FDA / FD&C Act jurisdiction), and plausibly members of the Science, Space, and Technology Committee given the AI/autonomy dimension.
* Prepare the pitch package the Member's staff will expect: a one‑page summary, a section‑by‑section analysis, a plain‑English problem/solution memo, the legislative findings, and a comparative print ("Ramseyer," showing changes to existing law) — VVUQ‑04 already produces something close to this.
* Line up original cosponsors (bipartisan support meaningfully improves a bill's prospects).
* Stakeholder engagement. Brief FDA/HHS, patient‑advocacy groups, industry, academic societies, and the standards bodies the bill leans on (ASME V&V 40, IEC 80601‑2‑77, ISO 13482, ISO/TS 15066). Keep all outreach consistent with the repo's "respectful and forward looking, not endorsed by" framing.

Phase 3 — Professional drafting and the 2026 pre‑introduction requirements
* Office of the Legislative Counsel (HOLC). The sponsoring office should route the text through the House Office of the Legislative Counsel (legcounsel.house.gov) to put it in proper legislative form. It isn't required by rule, but it is standard practice, it's nonpartisan and confidential, and "several drafts may be required before a measure is ready for formal introduction."
* Constitutional Authority Statement (CAS). Required by House Rule XII, clause 7(c) for every bill, submitted at introduction. For an FD&C Act amendment, the citation is the Commerce Clause (Art. I, §8, cl. 3), on which the FD&C Act rests.
* Sponsor's signature + cosponsor form on the Clerk's form.
* PAYGO / cost statements. Prepare a PAYGO statement if the bill has budgetary effects, and an earmark declaration (here, presumably negative). Informal CBO consultation can be sought, though formal scoring typically follows introduction.
* Anticipate committee referral. Expect primary referral to Energy and Commerce (Subcommittee on Health), with possible secondary referral. Drafting to a single, coherent subject helps — note that a federal single‑subject rule does not exist (the "One Subject at a Time Act," S. 59, is only a proposal), so this is good practice, not a requirement.

Phase 4 — The actual submission (introduction)
Once the above is done, "submission to Congress" is the moment of introduction:
* The Member (or staff) submits the signed PDF — bill text, CAS, and cosponsor form — via the eHopper, which the Clerk's office confirms is "now the House's primary way of introducing bills" (the physical hopper on the floor remains available). Submissions are only accepted from ~15 minutes before the House convenes through ~15 minutes after it adjourns.
* The Clerk assigns the real H.R. number, the Speaker (with the Parliamentarian) refers it to committee, and GPO prints it in introduced form.

2026‑specific timing and cautions
* Calendar window. The 119th Congress, 2nd Session is calendar year 2026, and the Congress ends January 3, 2027. Any bill not enacted by then dies and must be reintroduced in the 120th Congress — so the realistic target is introduction well inside 2026.
* AI‑authored origin. There is no House rule requiring disclosure that text was AI‑drafted, but given that these drafts were generated autonomously, be transparent with the sponsoring office and ensure a named human attorney/expert takes ownership of the final text before it is introduced.
* Keep the disclaimers true. Until a Member actually introduces it, the document remains an independent research draft — not pending legislation, not endorsed by the FDA or any Member, and not legal advice.
