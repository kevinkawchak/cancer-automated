Do Medical AI Bills Reference Code/Technical Attachments?
Yes, increasingly so. Unlike traditional legislation that only references other laws, emerging medical AI bills explicitly reference and require submission of actual algorithmic code and technical documentation. This represents a significant shift in legislative practice.
Real-World Examples
New York's Assembly Bill A9149 requires health insurers and HMOs to submit the actual artificial intelligence-based algorithms and training data sets being used in the utilization review process to the state Department of Financial Services, which then certifies that these algorithms and training data sets minimize bias risk based on protected characteristics and adhere to evidence-based clinical guidelines.
Texas's Senate Bill 1822 originally required insurers to submit the actual AI algorithm and its training datasets to the Texas Department of Insurance, though the committee substitute version modified this to require submission of algorithms only if the commissioner determines additional documentation is needed based on noncompliance concerns.
Materials Required Throughout Bill Development
Phase 1: Bill Drafting & Format Requirements
The U.S. House of Representatives has used an XML authoring environment for drafting legislation since September 2002, with the House planning to draft over 95% of bills in XML format. The United States Legislative Markup (USLM) is an XML information model designed to represent legislation of United States Congress, allowing for conversion of existing titles and support for drafting new bills with flexible versioning that can be managed hierarchically from document root down to individual provisions.
Technical Submission Formats:
* Primary format: USLM XML (current standard as of 2025)
* Alternative formats: Congress.gov provides legislative documents in HTML/XML, TXT, USLM, or PDF formats, with PDF providing complete and accurate display of legislative text and USLM being the optimal source.
* Legacy format: Bill DTD XML (still in use but being phased out)
Phase 2: Bill Components & Attachments
Medical AI bills typically reference or require attachment of:
1. Algorithm Documentation
    * The logic or decision tree of AI-based algorithms, description of each training data set used (including data sources), and documentation of the issuer's process for overseeing and validating the algorithm's performance and compliance.
2. Attestations & Compliance Statements
    * Statements attesting that algorithms and their training data sets have minimized bias risk based on race, color, religion, ancestry, age, sex, gender, national origin, or disability and comply with evidence-based clinical guidelines.
3. Prior Law References
    * Bills include specific citations to existing statutes (e.g., "Amends Section 4201.156, Insurance Code")
    * References to existing federal regulatory frameworks
Phase 3: Supporting Documentation (Not Physically Attached, But Referenced)
Medical AI bills typically reference several bodies of prior law:
Federal Regulatory Framework:
* HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act): HIPAA provides baseline federal standards for the protection of personal health information and covers a wide range of health information technology applications, including AI.
* FDA Software as Medical Device (SaMD) Regulations: FDA regulations for Software as a Medical Device require testing of the safety and efficacy of AI-enabled medical devices through a premarket submission program.
* 21st Century Cures Act (2016): This Act narrowed FDA's authority over certain health software by amending Section 520 of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act to exclude some clinical decision support software from being classified as medical devices if they support rather than replace clinical decision-making.
* 42 CFR Part 2 (Confidentiality of Substance Use Disorder Treatment Records): These regulations are referenced for entities receiving federal financial assistance and are being reconciled with HIPAA requirements in conjunction with AI policy development.
CMS & Medicare/Medicaid Framework:
* CMS can influence tool use through reimbursement policies, coverage decisions, and Conditions of Participation, though CoPs do not yet include AI-specific requirements but could mandate human oversight, algorithm transparency, or documentation.
Text/Code/PDF Format Requirements for Submission
Bill Text Format Specifications
Structural Requirements (USLM XML):
* Federal legislation is highly structured with sections as basic units, containing up to seven levels of hierarchy (subsection, paragraph, subparagraph, clause, subclause, item, subitem), and sections can be within seven higher levels (division, title, subtitle, chapter, subchapter, part, subpart).
* Bills include pre-form (metadata used during drafting), form (information about the bill such as official title), and the legislative body (actual content of the bill).
Algorithm/Code Submission Standards:
When bills specifically require algorithm submission (as in NY A9149 and Texas SB 1822):
* Standard formats: Not explicitly specified in bills; typically regulated agencies prescribe format
* Confidentiality protection: Information submitted to the department is confidential and not subject to disclosure under public records laws.
* Regulatory discretion: The form and manner of submission is prescribed by the state's commissioner of insurance, allowing regulatory agencies flexibility to require electronic or paper submissions, specific data formats, and documentation standards.
PDF vs. Formal Legislative Requirements
* PDF: Used for display and archival but is not the primary submission format for Congress
* XML (USLM): Required for modern legislative drafting and processing
* Plain text (.txt): Available for accessibility but formatting may be lost
Detailed Resource Inventory: Prior Laws & Regulatory Resources
Primary Legislative References in Medical AI Bills
State-Level Bills Reference:
1. Insurance Code provisions (state-specific chapters governing health plans)
2. Medical Practice Acts (licensing and practice standards)
3. State Privacy Laws (California Privacy Rights Act, Colorado's AI law, etc.)
Federal Laws Commonly Referenced:
1. 42 U.S.C. § 1395y - Medicare determinations of medical necessity and appropriateness
2. 45 CFR Parts 160, 164 - HIPAA Privacy and Security Rules
3. 21 CFR Part 860-889 - FDA Medical Device regulations
4. 26 U.S.C. - Federal antidiscrimination laws (Title VI, Americans with Disabilities Act provisions)
Regulatory Guidance (Referenced but Not Incorporated by Reference)
* FDA's January 2025 draft guidance on marketing submission recommendations for AI-enabled devices, establishing guiding principles for transparency in ML-enabled medical devices.
* The Assistant Secretary for Technology Policy under HHS requires certified health IT to meet certain transparency requirements for AI.
Industry Standards Referenced
Though not typically attached, bills often reference:
* NIST AI Risk Management Framework (referenced in Colorado's revised SB 205)
* Evidence-Based Clinical Guidelines (specific guidelines not named but referenced generally)
* URAC (Utilization Review Accreditation Commission) standards - Illinois H2472 requires UM programs using algorithmic automatic processes to comply with either URAC Health Utilization Management Standards or National Committee standards.
Bill Development Timeline & Documentation Flow
Pre-Filing Stage:
* Drafters work with legislative counsel
* Research on prior laws and regulations conducted
* Technical documentation (if required) outlined in bill language
Filing Stage:
* Bill submitted in USLM XML format to chamber clerk
* Text, PDF, and plain text versions generated automatically
* No physical code/algorithm files submitted with bill itself
Post-Passage/Regulatory Implementation Stage:
* Legislature delegates specific format/submission requirements to regulatory agencies
* Agencies then develop detailed guidance on algorithm submission formats, data protection, and technical specifications
Key Distinction
Important difference: The bills themselves don't contain or attach actual code. Rather, they mandate future submissionof code and algorithms to regulatory agencies under procedures the agencies develop. This allows bills to:
1. Maintain legislative simplicity
2. Allow regulatory agencies flexibility to update technical requirements
3. Protect proprietary information through agency confidentiality provisions
4. Adapt to evolving technical standards
Federal agencies can incorporate material by reference in legislative rules, allowing them to reference external standards, documents, and technical specifications without reproducing them in full within regulations.
This approach represents an evolution where legislation becomes increasingly technically prescriptive while preserving the traditional legislative document structure.
