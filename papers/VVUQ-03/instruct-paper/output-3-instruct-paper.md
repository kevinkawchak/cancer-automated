### Gemini 3.1 Pro

Author Rating: 8.0, Basic with some references

In U.S. legislative practice, bills themselves almost never contain executable source code. Instead, legislation is primarily composed of statutory language that defines standards, mandates oversight, or authorizes executive agencies (like the FDA or HHS) to create technical regulations.
While "code" in the programming sense is absent from the text of a bill, "codes" in the form of administrative identifiers (e.g., CPT or ICD-10) and technical frameworks are frequently referenced to anchor the law in the real-world medical infrastructure.
1. Do Bills Contain Executable Code?
No, U.S. medical AI bills do not include executable code or software attachments. Legislation is designed to be technology-neutral so that it remains relevant even as software evolves.
* Delegated Authority: Rather than including code, a bill will mandate that an agency (e.g., the FDA) develop "technical standards" for AI safety (Hirani et al., 2024).
* Referenced "Codes": Legislation often references Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) codes or International Classification of Diseases (ICD) codes to specify how AI-driven medical procedures should be billed or tracked (Zhu et al., 2024). For example, CMS has introduced specific CPT codes (like 92229 for autonomous retinal imaging) which are referenced in reimbursement-related legislation (Zhu et al., 2024).
2. Text and Attachments Needed During Development
Throughout the development of a medical AI bill, several non-statutory documents are "attached" or associated with the bill to provide technical context.
Required Documentation for Submission
To submit a bill in Congress, the primary requirement is the Legislative Text, but the process involves the following "attachments" in the legislative record:
* Sponsor's Introductory Statement: A formal explanation of why the AI regulation is necessary.
* Committee Reports: These are the most critical "attachments." They contain the Section-by-Section Analysis,which explains the technical intent of the law in plain English.
* CBO Cost Estimate: A fiscal report from the Congressional Budget Office detailing the cost of implementing the AI oversight.
* Agency Correspondence: Letters from the FDA or NIST detailing how they plan to implement the bill’s technical requirements (e.g., adopting the NIST AI Risk Management Framework) (Federal Legislative History Research,n.d.).
Technical Resources Included with Text
Legislators often incorporate external technical resources by reference rather than by inclusion:
* NIST AI Risk Management Framework (RMF): Recent federal bills, such as the Federal Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Act of 2024, explicitly require agencies and vendors to adopt the NIST AI RMF (Federal AI Risk Management Act, 2024).
* ISO/IEC Standards: Bills may reference international standards (like ISO/IEC 42001) to define what constitutes an "AI system."
3. Format Requirements for Submission
The U.S. Government Publishing Office (GPO) and the House/Senate Parliamentarians enforce strict formatting for bill submission:
Requirement	Specification
Primary Format	XML (United States Legislative Markup - USLM) is the standard for digital drafting to ensure interoperability.
Submission Format	Members typically submit a signed physical copy and an electronic version (PDF/Word) to the "Hopper" (House) or Clerk (Senate).
Structural Units	Must follow a hierarchy: Title > Subtitle > Section > Subsection > Paragraph > Subparagraph.
Drafting Tools	Most bills are drafted using the Legislative Information System (LIS) or the House/Senate Legislative Counsel’s specialized drafting software.
4. Prior Laws and Included Technical Resources
Historically, medical AI has been regulated by adapting existing frameworks rather than creating "code-dependent" laws from scratch.
* 21st Century Cures Act (2016): This is the foundation for U.S. medical software regulation. It did not include code but clarified which types of "Clinical Decision Support" (CDS) software are exempt from FDA device regulation.
* Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (FDCA): As amended, it empowers the FDA to enforce safety for "Software as a Medical Device" (SaMD) (Health Care AI: Law, Regulation, and Policy, 2022).
* State-Level Laws (e.g., California/Connecticut): Recent state laws (2023–2024) require "Impact Assessments." While they don't attach code, they require developers to submit Data Disclosure and Bias Testing results to state agencies for review (Artificial Intelligence in Government, 2024).
