% SECTION 2. FINDINGS.
% ============================================================
\billsec{SEC. 2.}{FINDINGS.}

\uscprov{1}{\textbf{(a) Findings.} Congress finds the following:}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(1)} Physical AI oncology clinical investigations place
autonomous and semi-autonomous robots in direct physical contact with patients,
and the behavior of such a robot at the moment of contact is determined by
software that is increasingly machine-generated \cite{kawchak2026national}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(2)} No Federal law requires the verification of
robot-patient interaction code to clear before that code is generated or
executed; a sponsor may generate the code, execute it, and document afterward,
because the order of operations is not regulated \cite{kawchak2026vvuq01}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(3)} Embodiment raises the stakes from software quality to
patient safety: in a disembodied pipeline an error yields a wrong number a human
can catch downstream, while in an embodied surgical system the same error class
yields a wrong motion a human cannot catch in time \cite{kawchak2026vvuq01}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(4)} The predetermined change control plan authority in
section 515C of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act (21 U.S.C. 360e-4) and
the Food and Drug Administration final guidance on such plans for artificial
intelligence-enabled device software functions are the closest existing
analogues to validating a change before deployment, but each remains voluntary,
submission-based, and neither automated nor required in advance of generation
\cite{fda-pccp-2024}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(5)} The Quality Management System Regulation (part 820 of
title 21, Code of Federal Regulations), effective February 2, 2026, and the
electronic-records requirements of part 11 of such title supply quality and
record-integrity scaffolding but do not impose a rule that verification precede
generation \cite{fr-qmsr-2024}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(6)} A recognized consensus standard, ASME V\&V 40-2018, and
the Food and Drug Administration guidance assessing the credibility of
computational modeling establish a credibility framework, in which model risk is
the product of model influence and decision consequence, that an automated gate
can implement \cite{asmevv40, fda-credibility-2023}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(7)} Recognized consensus standards, including NASA-STD-7009A,
IEEE Std 7009-2024, IEC 80601-2-77, ISO 14971, and ISO/TS 15066, supply the
dispersion bounds, fail-safe requirements, and biomechanical contact limits to
which such a gate may be bound \cite{nasastd7009, isots15066}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(8)} Automated verification of robot-patient interaction code
is feasible today: a pipeline held the verification fraction at 1.0, accepting
one candidate artifact and blocking five, and a ten-gate assurance suite over
control software for a surgical humanoid cleared reproducibly from a fixed seed,
each gate bound to a published external standard \cite{kawchak2026vvuq01,
kawchak2026vvuq02}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(9)} Human-over-AI review is already widely adopted in State
law, including the California Physicians Make Decisions Act (Senate Bill 1120),
the Illinois Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources Act (House Bill
1806), the Texas Responsible Artificial Intelligence Governance Act (House Bill
149), and Maryland House Bill 820, each preserving a licensed human's authority
over an artificial-intelligence-influenced decision \cite{ca-sb1120, il-hb1806,
tx-hb149, md-hb820}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(10)} The strongest Federal precedent for mandatory
human-over-AI oversight is the Medicare Advantage artificial-intelligence
guardrail (section 422.101(c) of title 42, Code of Federal Regulations), under
which an algorithm alone may not justify a coverage denial \cite{cms-4201-ma}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(11)} Federal nondiscrimination law, including section 92.210
of title 45, Code of Federal Regulations, title VI of the Civil Rights Act of
1964 (42 U.S.C. 2000d), and the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (42
U.S.C. 12101 et seq.), requires that a patient-facing algorithm minimize the
risk of bias and respect accessibility \cite{cfr-92-210, usc-titlevi, usc-ada}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(12)} The artificial-intelligence taxonomy maintained by the
American Medical Association, distinguishing assistive, augmentative, and
autonomous functions, provides a graduated spine for matching verification rigor
to the degree of autonomy of a device \cite{cpt-appendix-s}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(13)} A Physical AI system is at once a medical product and a
research tool, and a requirement that verification precede generation is
agnostic to that classification, because whatever the system is called, its
patient-facing code must clear the gate before it runs \cite{kawchak2026national}.}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(14)} As clinical investigation moves toward real-time and
decentralized designs governed by harmonized good clinical practice, manual,
document-by-document review cannot keep pace with the volume of machine-generated
control code, and an automated gate that clears before generation serves rather
than impedes that modernization \cite{iche6r3}.}

\billsec{SEC. 3.}{AMENDMENT TO THE FEDERAL FOOD, DRUG, AND COSMETIC ACT.}

\uscprov{1}{\textbf{(a) In General.} Subchapter V of the Federal Food, Drug, and
Cosmetic Act (21 U.S.C. 351 et seq.) is amended by inserting after section 515C
(21 U.S.C. 360e-4) the following new section:}

\uscquote{0}{``SEC. 515D. VERIFICATION BEFORE GENERATION OF ROBOT-PATIENT
INTERACTION CODE IN PHYSICAL AI ONCOLOGY INVESTIGATIONS.}
\uscquote{0}{``(a) Requirement.- No robot-patient interaction code may be
generated or executed in a Physical AI oncology clinical investigation unless an
automated verification, validation, and uncertainty quantification process,
bound to one or more named external standards, has first cleared for that
interaction, and the cleared verification record has been documented and
attested under subsection (e).}
\uscquote{0}{``(b) Order of Operations.- A sponsor shall not generate, and shall
not execute, robot-patient interaction code in advance of the cleared
verification record. A record created after generation or execution does not
satisfy this section.}
\uscquote{0}{``(c) Gate Thresholds and Decision Rule.-}
\uscquote{1}{``(1) In general.- For each robot-patient interaction, the
verification process shall apply the gate thresholds set out in the schedule in
paragraph (4).}
\uscquote{1}{``(2) Decision rule.- Each gate shall emit a determination of
ACCEPT, BLOCK, or ESCALATE. A single failing dimension shall set BLOCK, and
ambiguity or divergence shall set ESCALATE and return the interaction to a
qualified human.}
\uscquote{1}{``(3) Conditions.- For each interaction the verification fraction
shall equal 1.0, so that every verification check passes; the validation
agreement shall meet or exceed the per-gate threshold, with the maximum relative
error at or below the per-gate bound and the human review recorded; the
coefficient of variation across not fewer than three seeded runs shall not
exceed the per-gate bound; and, for an interaction subject to a hard catastrophe
predicate, that predicate shall also hold.}
\uscquote{1}{``(4) Threshold schedule.- The per-gate agreement and dispersion
bounds, the gates that carry a hard catastrophe predicate, and the external
standard that governs each gate are as follows:}

\uscquote{0}{``(d) Readiness Gates.- Before a patient is enrolled or a robot
acts, the trial site shall pass the Physical AI Standard Level gate on
legislative authorization, regulatory compliance, and operational readiness;
each robot shall meet the minimum Unification Standard Level for its functional
type, which is not less than 7.0 for a surgical type, not less than 6.0 for a
therapeutic positioning or diagnostic needle placement type, not less than 4.0
for a rehabilitative type, and not less than 3.0 for a companion monitoring
type; and each robot's behavior shall be validated across not fewer than two
simulation frameworks within the cross-framework tolerances of less than 2
millimeters of trajectory deviation and less than 0.5 newton of force
discrepancy.}
\uscquote{0}{``(e) Documentation and Attestation.- Before robot-patient
interaction code is generated or executed, the sponsor shall make and file the
following:}
\uscquote{1}{``(1) Algorithm documentation.- For each covered algorithm,
documentation that includes a plain-language summary of its function, a
description of its decision logic or architecture, a description of the data sets
used to train or condition it, the gate bindings identifying the external
standard that governs each gate and the threshold enforced, the
verification-before-generation record showing the gate cleared before the code
was generated or executed, and a determinism statement giving the seed and
confirming reproducible regeneration.}
\uscquote{1}{``(2) Compliance statement and attestation.- A compliance statement
and a signed attestation by a responsible officer of the sponsor certifying
that the automated verification process cleared before any covered code was
generated or executed, that each gate met its threshold bound to a named
external standard, that the trial site passed its Physical AI Standard Level gate
and each robot met its Unification Standard Level minimum, that the algorithm and
its training data minimize the risk of bias on protected characteristics and
adhere to evidence-based clinical guidelines, and that a licensed human retained
the medical-necessity decision.}
\uscquote{1}{``(3) Record integrity.- The records required by this subsection
shall be maintained in a hash-chained, tamper-evident audit trail that satisfies
part 11 of title 21, Code of Federal Regulations.}
\uscquote{0}{``(f) Cybersecurity, Human Oversight, and Lifecycle.- Each Physical
AI system shall incorporate authentication and access control, encryption of
data in transit and at rest, network segmentation, and software-integrity
verification; shall operate under recorded human oversight with a manual override
and an emergency stop that brings the system to a safe state within the
procedure-appropriate budget and a hand-back-to-human escalation when a gate
escalates or a fault is detected; and shall maintain configuration and
model-version management, monitor for model and data drift, and, on
decommissioning, archive the audit trail, securely erase patient data, and
revoke access.}
\uscquote{0}{``(g) Nondiscrimination.- Each covered algorithm and its training
data shall minimize the risk of bias on protected characteristics and adhere to
evidence-based clinical guidelines, and each Physical AI system shall be designed
and operated consistent with applicable accessibility and accommodation
requirements.}
\uscquote{0}{``(h) Regulations.- Not later than 365 days after the date of
enactment of this section, the Secretary shall issue final regulations to carry
out this section. Pending the issuance of those regulations, the Secretary may
issue interim guidance, and a sponsor may comply with this section by meeting the
requirements of subsections (a) through (g).}
\uscquote{0}{``(i) Definitions.- In this section:}
\uscquote{1}{``(1) The term `Physical AI system' means an artificial-intelligence
system operating in the physical world through a robotic platform, comprising
hardware, control software, embedded models, and sensors.}
\uscquote{1}{``(2) The term `robot agent' means an autonomous Physical AI system
executing trial procedures through protocol-mediated data access under a defined
workflow.}
\uscquote{1}{``(3) The term `robot-patient interaction code' means any algorithm
or generated software that governs a Physical AI system's physical contact with,
or action upon, a human subject, whether the system is a medical product, a
research tool, or both.}
\uscquote{1}{``(4) The term `generative artificial intelligence' means an
artificial-intelligence system that produces novel content, including text,
code, or robot action sequences, from learned patterns.}
\uscquote{1}{``(5) The term `AI-enabled device software function' means a
software function, within or constituting a device, that uses a machine-learning
model to achieve its intended purpose.}
\uscquote{1}{``(6) The terms `verification', `validation', and `uncertainty
quantification' mean, respectively, confirmation that an implementation conforms
to its specification, confirmation that a result agrees with an independent
reference of reality, and measurement of how confident a result is across runs,
expressed as statistical dispersion.}
\uscquote{1}{``(7) The term `verification fraction' means the fraction of
verification checks that pass, defined as an equality to 1.0.}
\uscquote{1}{``(8) The term `validation agreement' means agreement with an
independent reference at or above a per-gate threshold, with the maximum relative
error at or below a per-gate bound and the human review recorded.}
\uscquote{1}{``(9) The term `coefficient-of-variation bound' means the maximum
allowed ratio of standard deviation to mean across not fewer than three seeded
runs.}
\uscquote{1}{``(10) The term `hard catastrophe predicate' means an additional
pass-fail condition on an immediate-catastrophe interaction, such as zero breach
of a vascular no-fly volume, a minimum human clearance, or a guaranteed safe
state, that must also hold.}
\uscquote{1}{``(11) The term `Physical AI Standard Level' means a site-level
compliance gate scored pass or fail across legislative authorization, regulatory
compliance, and operational readiness.}
\uscquote{1}{``(12) The term `Unification Standard Level' means a robot-level
readiness score from 1.0 to 10.0 computed across simulation-framework switching,
artificial-intelligence integration, cross-robot sharing, and clinical-trial
collaboration.}
\uscquote{1}{``(13) The term `hash-chained audit trail' means an immutable,
tamper-evident, cryptographically linked record of every system action.}
\uscquote{0}{``(j) Rule of Construction.- Nothing in this section displaces the
investigational-device exemption under section 520(g), the protection of human
subjects under part 50 of title 21, Code of Federal Regulations, the
investigational new drug framework under part 312 of such title, or the
predetermined change control plan authority under section 515C as it applies
outside Physical AI, and nothing in this section itself reclassifies any
device.''.}

\uscprov{1}{\textbf{(b) Conforming Amendments.} The Federal Food, Drug, and
Cosmetic Act is amended as follows, with the corresponding changes shown in the
comparative print in section 4 of this Act:}

\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(1)} Section 201(h) (21 U.S.C. 321(h)) is amended in the
concluding matter of paragraph (1) by inserting after the sentence relating to
software functions excluded under section 520(o) a sentence providing that a
software function that generates or executes robot-patient interaction code, or
that directs the autonomous physical action of a robot in contact with a human
subject, is a device and is not so excluded.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(2)} Section 301 (21 U.S.C. 331) is amended by adding at the
end a new prohibited act reaching the generation or execution of robot-patient
interaction code in violation of section 515D and the failure to make or file the
required documentation and attestation.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(3)} Section 501 (21 U.S.C. 351) is amended by adding a new
subsection deeming adulterated a Physical AI device software function used to
generate or execute robot-patient interaction code without a cleared section
515D verification record.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(4)} Section 505F (21 U.S.C. 355g) is amended to recognize
records generated under section 515D as real world evidence and to apply the
program to devices in Physical AI oncology investigations.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(5)} Section 510(k) (21 U.S.C. 360(k)) is amended so a
verification-governed change to a Physical AI device consistent with an
established predetermined change control plan does not require a new premarket
notification.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(6)} Section 513 (21 U.S.C. 360c) is amended to graduate
special controls for a device that generates or executes robot-patient
interaction code by its degree of autonomy.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(7)} Section 515 (21 U.S.C. 360e) is amended so a premarket
approval application for such a device must include the cleared verification
record and gate evidence required by section 515D.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(8)} Section 515C (21 U.S.C. 360e-4) is amended so a
predetermined change control plan for such a device must include an automated
verification-before-change protocol consistent with section 515D.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(9)} Section 520(o) (21 U.S.C. 360j(o)) is amended to confirm
that such a software function is not within the clinical-decision-support
exclusion and remains a device.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(10)} Section 521 (21 U.S.C. 360k) is amended by adding a
savings clause preserving State human-in-the-loop and audit requirements that are
not different from, or in addition to, a Federal device requirement.}

\uscprov{1}{\textbf{(c) Clerical Amendment.} The table of contents for the
Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act is amended by inserting after the item
relating to section 515C the following new item:}

\uscquote{0}{``Sec. 515D. Verification before generation of robot-patient
interaction code in Physical AI oncology investigations.''.}

\uscprov{1}{\textbf{(d) Rule of Construction.} Nothing in this Act, or the
amendments made by this Act, shall be construed to displace the
investigational-device exemption under section 520(g) of the Federal Food, Drug,
and Cosmetic Act (21 U.S.C. 360j(g)), the protection of human subjects under part
50 of title 21, Code of Federal Regulations, the investigational new drug
framework under part 312 of such title, the predetermined change control plan
authority under section 515C of such Act as it applies outside Physical AI, or
any State law preserved under section 521 of such Act as amended by this Act;
and nothing in this Act reclassifies any device.}

\uscprov{1}{\textbf{(e) Effective Date; Implementation Deadline; Transition
Rule.}}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(1)} \textbf{Effective date.} This Act and the amendments
made by this Act shall take effect on the first day of the first calendar quarter
beginning not less than one year after the date of enactment of Act.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(2)} \textbf{Implementation deadline.} The Secretary shall
issue the final regulations required by section 515D(h) of the Federal Food,
Drug, and Cosmetic Act not later than 365 days after the date of enactment of
this Act.}
\uscprov{2}{\textbf{(3)} \textbf{Transition rule.} A Physical AI oncology
clinical investigation already enrolling on the effective date shall have 180
days after that date to come into compliance with section 515D, and this Act
does not disturb the February 2, 2026, effective date of the Quality Management
System Regulation.}
