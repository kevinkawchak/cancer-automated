A good submission should be an amendatory bill, not a rewrite of all Title 21. “Public Law 119-93” appears to be the current U.S. Code release point for Title 21, not the name of the food-and-drug law being amended; the FDA states that the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act and later amendments are codified in Title 21, chapter 9, and OLRC lists Title 21 as current through Public Law 119-93.  ￼ Because Title 21 is not shown as a positive-law title on OLRC’s download page, the safer drafting form is: “The Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act is amended…”, with the U.S.C. cite in parentheses as a reader aid. HOLC’s drafting guide says non-positive-law titles should generally be amended by citing the underlying statute, not the Code section alone.  ￼

Direct answers

Do not include non-relevant sections in the bill text. Do not “remove” anything unless the policy is to repeal it. Use precise amendatory instructions such as “is amended by inserting after…” or “is amended by adding at the end…”. House drafting guidance treats the bill section as the basic unit and distinguishes amendatory instructions “outside the quotes” from new statutory text “inside the quotes.”  ￼

Do not abbreviate non-relevant sections in enacted text. Ellipses, “[no change],” and abbreviated headings belong in an explanatory memo, comparison print, or table of affected provisions—not in operative bill language.

Use a table of context outside the bill, or in a short “section-by-section summary.” A bill can include a table of contents for its own sections, but it should not include a table of every unaffected Title 21 heading.

Use headings only for what the bill actually changes. For a focused update, call the bill something like “Physical AI Oncology Clinical Trial Implementation Act of 2026.” The new statutory section could be titled “Physical artificial intelligence systems in oncology clinical investigations.” If the draft amends both drug/biologic trial provisions and device provisions, a cross-cutting placement in FD&C Act chapter V, part E is more coherent than scattering text throughout unrelated Title 21 chapters; FDA’s chapter V table shows Part E as “General Provisions Relating to Drugs and Devices.”

FDA already has active policy work on AI, digital health technologies, decentralized clinical trials, and AI-enabled devices. That means the bill should define the new physical-AI onco ￼d set minimum statutory guardrails rather than duplicate guidance. FDA’s AI drug-development guidance uses a risk-based credibility framework for context of use, FDA’s DHT program covers remote trial data acquisition and decentralized trial modernization, and FDA maintains a public AI-enabled medical-device list for transparency.

Here is a submission-ready skeleton for the bill text and headings:

A BILL

To amend the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act to establish requirements and a pilot program for the use of physical artificial intelligence systems in oncology clinical investigations, and for other purposes.

SECTION 1. SHORT TITLE; TABLE OF CONTENTS.

(a) SHORT TITLE.—This Act may be cited as the “Physical AI Oncology Clinical Trial Implementation Act of 2026”.

(b) TABLE OF CONTENTS.—The table of contents for this Act is as follows:

Sec. 1. Short title; table of contents.
Sec. 2. Definitions.
Sec. 3. Physical artificial intelligence systems in oncology clinical investigations.
Sec. 4. Conforming amendments.
Sec. 5. Guidance and regulations.
Sec. 6. Report to Congress.
Sec. 7. Rule of construction.
Sec. 8. Effective date.

SEC. 2. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

(1) ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE.—The term “artificial intelligence” has the meaning given such term in [insert applicable Federal cross-reference].

(2) ONCOLOGY CLINICAL INVESTIGATION.—The term “oncology clinical investigation” means a clinical investigation regulated by the Food and Drug Administration that evaluates a drug, biological product, device, combination product, diagnostic, digital health technology, or other medical product for the diagnosis, monitoring, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of cancer or a cancer-related condition.

(3) PHYSICAL ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE SYSTEM.—The term “physical artificial intelligence system” means an artificial intelligence-enabled system that—
(A) acquires data from a patient, clinical environment, medical device, laboratory instrument, imaging system, robotic system, sensor, wearable, implantable, or other physical source;
(B) processes such data to generate a recommendation, classification, prediction, control instruction, workflow action, dose-related output, treatment-related output, monitoring output, or trial-operation output; and
(C) is intended for use in, or to support, an oncology clinical investigation.

(4) SECRETARY.—The term “Secretary” means the Secretary of Health and Human Services, acting through the Commissioner of Food and Drugs.

SEC. 3. PHYSICAL ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE SYSTEMS IN ONCOLOGY CLINICAL INVESTIGATIONS.

Chapter V of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act is amended by inserting after section [569C] the following:

“SEC. [569D]. PHYSICAL ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE SYSTEMS IN ONCOLOGY CLINICAL INVESTIGATIONS.

“(a) IN GENERAL.—The Secretary shall establish a framework for the safe, effective, transparent, and scientifically valid use of physical artificial intelligence systems in oncology clinical investigations.

“(b) SCOPE.—This section applies to the use of a physical artificial intelligence system in an oncology clinical investigation if the system is used to—
“(1) identify, screen, recruit, stratify, monitor, or retain participants;
“(2) acquire, generate, transform, interpret, or transmit trial data;
“(3) support assessment of eligibility, safety, efficacy, disease progression, response, adverse events, or clinical endpoints;
“(4) control, guide, or assist a robotic, imaging, radiation, infusion, laboratory, diagnostic, sensor, wearable, implantable, or other physical system used in the investigation; or
“(5) otherwise materially affect the rights, safety, welfare, treatment, or trial participation of a participant.

“(c) SPONSOR SUBMISSION REQUIREMENTS.—A sponsor that proposes to use a physical artificial intelligence system in an oncology clinical investigation shall submit to the Secretary, in such form and manner as the Secretary may require—
“(1) a statement of the system’s context of use;
“(2) a description of the system architecture, input data, output data, intended users, physical components, and operational environment;
“(3) evidence supporting model credibility, validation, performance, and limitations for the proposed context of use;
“(4) a risk management plan addressing patient safety, cybersecurity, data integrity, model drift, human factors, bias, failure modes, and foreseeable misuse;
“(5) a human oversight plan identifying when human review, override, confirmation, escalation, or shutdown is required;
“(6) a data governance plan addressing provenance, quality, representativeness, privacy, auditability, access controls, and retention;
“(7) a change management plan for modifications to models, software, hardware, sensors, interfaces, or operating conditions during the investigation;
“(8) a monitoring and adverse-event reporting plan specific to physical artificial intelligence system performance; and
“(9) such other information as the Secretary determines necessary to protect participants and ensure reliable regulatory decision-making.

“(d) PATIENT PROTECTIONS.—The Secretary shall require that each oncology clinical investigation subject to this section include safeguards to ensure—
“(1) informed consent that clearly discloses the role of the physical artificial intelligence system;
“(2) appropriate institutional review board review;
“(3) timely notice to investigators and participants of material system changes or newly identified risks;
“(4) meaningful human supervision for any output that may affect treatment, safety, eligibility, dosing, endpoint assessment, or trial continuation;
“(5) protections for participants with limited digital access, disability, language barriers, or other access needs; and
“(6) procedures to prevent the use of a system that presents an unreasonable risk to participants.

“(e) TRANSPARENCY.—The Secretary may require public disclosure, in a form that protects trade secrets and confidential commercial information, of information sufficient for patients, investigators, clinicians, and regulators to understand the role, intended use, limitations, and oversight of a physical artificial intelligence system used in an oncology clinical investigation.

“(f) PILOT PROGRAM.—The Secretary shall establish a voluntary pilot program to evaluate the use of physical artificial intelligence systems in oncology clinical investigations, including multicenter, decentralized, remote-monitoring, imaging, robotics, digital-twin, and real-world-data-supported trial designs.

“(g) CONSULTATION.—In carrying out this section, the Secretary shall consult with the Oncology Center of Excellence, the Center for Drug Evaluation and Research, the Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research, the Center for Devices and Radiological Health, the Office of Combination Products, the Digital Health Center of Excellence, patients, clinicians, sponsors, trial sites, statisticians, ethicists, cybersecurity experts, and other interested parties.

“(h) RULE OF CONSTRUCTION.—Nothing in this section shall be construed to—
“(1) limit the authority of the Secretary under any other provision of this Act or the Public Health Service Act;
“(2) deem any drug, biological product, device, combination product, or physical artificial intelligence system to be approved, licensed, cleared, authorized, or exempt from otherwise applicable requirements;
“(3) alter requirements for informed consent, institutional review board review, investigational new drug applications, investigational device exemptions, good clinical practice, adverse-event reporting, or protection of human subjects; or
“(4) require the disclosure of trade secrets or confidential commercial information.

“(i) GUIDANCE.—Not later than [18 months] after the date of enactment of this section, the Secretary shall issue draft guidance, and not later than [36 months] after such date of enactment shall issue final guidance, regarding implementation of this section.

“(j) REGULATIONS.—The Secretary may issue regulations to carry out this section.”.

SEC. 4. CONFORMING AMENDMENTS.

(a) TABLE OF CONTENTS.—The table of contents in section 1 of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act is amended by inserting after the item relating to section [569C] the following:

“Sec. [569D]. Physical artificial intelligence systems in oncology clinical investigations.”.

(b) ADDITIONAL CONFORMING AMENDMENTS.—[Insert any conforming amendments to sections 505(i), 520(g), 561, 569C, or other affected provisions, as determined necessary by Legislative Counsel.]

SEC. 5. GUIDANCE AND REGULATIONS.

In issuing guidance or regulations under section [569D] of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, as added by section 3, the Secretary shall address, at a minimum—
(1) model credibility and validation for stated contexts of use;
(2) human oversight and override;
(3) cybersecurity and data integrity;
(4) physical-system failure modes and safety controls;
(5) change control and lifecycle management;
(6) performance across relevant demographic and clinical subgroups;
(7) decentralized and remote trial elements;
(8) use of digital health technologies, sensors, robotics, imaging systems, and digital twins;
(9) adverse-event reporting and performance monitoring; and
(10) public transparency.

SEC. 6. REPORT TO CONGRESS.

Not later than [3 years] after the date of enactment of this Act, and every [2 years] thereafter through fiscal year [2032], the Secretary shall submit to the Committee on Energy and Commerce of the House of Representatives and the Committee on Health, Education, Labor, and Pensions of the Senate a report on implementation of section [569D] of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, including—
(1) the number and type of oncology clinical investigations using physical artificial intelligence systems;
(2) safety, data integrity, and trial-operation issues identified;
(3) actions taken to protect participants;
(4) recommendations for additional legislation; and
(5) recommendations for harmonizing requirements across drugs, biological products, devices, and combination products.

SEC. 7. RULE OF CONSTRUCTION.

Nothing in this Act, or an amendment made by this Act, shall be construed to affect the authority of the Secretary under the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, the Public Health Service Act, or any regulation promulgated under either Act, except as expressly provided.

SEC. 8. EFFECTIVE DATE.
